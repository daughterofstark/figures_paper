# STRIDE: hierarchy-aware reproducibility analysis for mechanism discovery from replicated molecular dynamics

*A downstream framework that reports mechanism claims at the spatial scale replicate evidence supports, validated on triplicate molecular dynamics of the dengue DENV1–4 NS2B–NS3 protease.*

---

## Abstract

Molecular-dynamics (MD) simulations are routinely mined for the residue, loop, or domain that "drives" a functional motion, but a claim read from a single trajectory carries a reproducibility uncertainty that is rarely quantified: the highest-coupled residue in one run need not recur in another. We describe STRIDE, a downstream analysis layer that consumes replicate-resolved per-residue effect fields and, at each level of a residue → secondary-structure → motif → domain → chain hierarchy, estimates what fraction of a region's effect is reproducible signal once between-replicate disagreement and autocorrelation-corrected sampling noise are separated. This fraction is summarized by a reproducibility coefficient ρ; STRIDE additionally reports a signed effect with false-discovery control, a between- versus within-replicate variance budget, and a directional-coherence summary, and compares conditions with the condition (here, serotype) as the unit of replication. It does not detect cavities, build networks, or estimate Markov states; it is a reproducibility layer that sits on top of the effect fields such methods produce. We validate it on triplicate all-atom MD of the complete NS2B–NS3 protease from all four dengue serotypes. Residue-level reproducibility is heterogeneous and distributed rather than concentrated at the catalytic core; it improves once residues are aggregated and largely saturates above the residue scale; the catalytic domains are directionally coherent but not the highest-ρ regions; robust signed effects are widespread rather than catalytic-localized; cross-serotype reproducibility is position-specific with only a limited shared core; and between-replicate variance dominates the uncertainty budget almost everywhere. Because the threshold that would turn ρ into a pass/fail decision is not calibrated on this dataset, ρ is treated as a continuous score and residue-scale statements are exploratory at three replicates, with interpretive weight on domain- and region-scale claims. Replicate-aware, hierarchy-aware analysis changes what can responsibly be claimed from MD-derived mechanism discovery, replacing unstable single-residue verdicts with scale-appropriate, uncertainty-quantified regional claims.

---

## Author summary

Simulations of proteins in motion are often used to name "the residue that matters" for a functional event such as the opening of a catalytic pocket. If that residue changes when the simulation is repeated, the claim describes one run rather than the protein. STRIDE is an analysis layer that takes several independent replicate simulations and, instead of naming a single driver, measures how reproducible an effect is at each level of biological organization — from individual residues up to structural domains — while keeping three quantities distinct that are easily conflated: how large an effect is, how consistent its direction is, and how reproducible it is across replicates. Applied to the protease of all four dengue viruses, a conserved and heavily pursued antiviral target, STRIDE shows that the strongest single-run signals do not reliably reproduce, that reproducibility is spread across the protein rather than localized to the catalytic site, that catalytic regions are distinguished by directional consistency more than by reproducibility, and that the main source of uncertainty is disagreement between independent runs rather than sampling within a run. The practical consequence is that with a handful of short replicates, MD-derived mechanism claims are more trustworthy when made at the regional level and checked across replicates, and STRIDE provides a transparent, reproducible way to do this.

## 1. Introduction

Molecular dynamics turns a static structure into a time-resolved account of motion, and a common use is mechanistic nomination: identifying the residue, loop, or domain whose movement is coupled to a functional observable such as a cavity volume, a distance, or a collective coordinate. The prevailing analysis computes a per-residue effect statistic on one trajectory and reports its extremum — the residue with the largest coupling — as the mechanism. This is a residue-resolution claim carried by a single sample of a stochastic process. Repeating the simulation from independent initial conditions need not return the same residue, the same direction of coupling, or even the same subregion; the reproducibility uncertainty of the claim is real but usually unstated.

The field already recognizes that replication matters. Community best-practice guidance recommends several independent simulations with statistical analysis [1,2], and autocorrelation-aware uncertainty estimation — block averaging [4], integrated autocorrelation times [3], effective sample sizes [5] — is well established. What remains awkward in practice is the step from "we ran replicates" to "here is the claim the replicates support, at the resolution they support it." A per-residue effect can be large in every run yet unstable in which residue leads; it can be reproducible in magnitude while flipping in direction; and it can be irreproducible at the residue scale yet stable once read at the scale of the enclosing loop or domain. Turning these observations into a decision — what to claim, and at what spatial scale — is typically left to author judgment.

Three distinctions organize the approach we take. First, reproducibility is not effect size: a strong effect that disagrees across replicates is not a reproducible mechanism. Second, directional coherence is not reproducibility: a region whose residues agree on the sign of their effect can still vary in magnitude across replicates, and vice versa. Third, spatial scale is a variable, not a fixed choice: the finest scale at which a claim is supportable depends on how much replicate evidence exists, so a method that always reports residue-level verdicts over-resolves by construction. Keeping these three apart, rather than fusing them into a single "importance" score, is what allows an analysis to state honestly where a mechanism is reproducible and at what resolution.

STRIDE operationalizes this as a downstream layer. It does not simulate, detect cavities, build residue-interaction networks, or estimate Markov state models; each of those has mature, well-cited tools, and STRIDE consumes rather than replaces their output. Its input is an effect field — a per-residue, per-replicate scalar coupling to a functional observable — and its output is, for each region and scale, a reproducibility coefficient, a signed effect with uncertainty, a variance decomposition, and a coherence summary, together with a cross-condition comparison that respects the unit of replication. Because it treats the effect functional as a black box, the same machinery applies whether the per-residue effect comes from a correlation to a cavity coordinate, a linear-response coefficient, or a network centrality.

We validate STRIDE on the dengue NS2B–NS3 protease. The NS2B–NS3 protease is essential to flaviviral polyprotein maturation and is a leading pan-serotype antiviral target; a useful therapeutic must engage all four serotypes (DENV1–4) [TK-DENV]. The four proteases are highly conserved in sequence and near-superimposable in static structure [TK-DENV], so average structural descriptors discriminate them poorly and attention has turned to dynamics — the NS2B-cofactor-dependent breathing of the catalytic and allosteric sub-pockets — as the axis on which the serotypes might differ. This makes the system a demanding test: a conserved target where any dynamics-based mechanistic claim is consequential and where the pull toward a clean catalytic-triad or single-residue story is strong. In earlier single-trajectory-style analysis of these same simulations, the top per-residue drivers were not stable across replicates — their identity, direction, and location varied within a serotype about as much as between serotypes (Supplementary Note S1). That instability is the motivation for the present framework, and the dengue results below are the demonstration of what a reproducibility- and scale-aware reading yields in its place.

## 2. Conceptual overview of STRIDE

### 2.1 The input: a replicated effect field

For each independent replicate k of a system, and each residue i, STRIDE takes a scalar effect θ that measures how strongly residue i couples to a chosen functional observable in that replicate. In the dengue case study, θ is the signed correlation between a residue's distance to the catalytic centre and the catalytic-pocket volume, with the sign retained so that outward-opening ("puller") and inward-opening ("wedge") couplings are distinguished. Nothing downstream depends on this particular choice: any per-residue, per-replicate scalar effect can be supplied.

### 2.2 Why replicate variation is the crux

If a residue's effect is large in one run but disagrees across independent runs, the mechanism it implies is not reproducible, however striking any single run appears. The central task is therefore to separate, for each region, the part of the effect that is stable across replicates from the part that merely reflects run-to-run wandering or finite sampling within a run.

### 2.3 The variance components

For a region R (a residue, or a group of residues at some scale), STRIDE models the regional effect measured in replicate k as a reproducible mean plus two independent sources of variation:

- **β** — the reproducible signed effect, the part of the effect that is shared across replicates;
- **τ²** — the between-replicate variance, the disagreement between independent simulations;
- **σ̄²** — the within-replicate sampling variance, corrected for temporal autocorrelation through an effective sample size (frames divided by twice the integrated autocorrelation time) [5], so that correlated frames are not miscounted as independent evidence.

Keeping τ² and σ̄² apart is deliberate: they have different remedies. Large σ̄² with small τ² means each run is under-sampled and longer runs would help; large τ² means the runs genuinely disagree, and longer single runs will not fix it — more independent replicates are needed.

### 2.4 The reproducibility coefficient ρ

These combine into a single interpretable score,

ρ = β² / (β² + τ² + σ̄²),   ρ between 0 and 1,

the fraction of a region's total effect "budget" that is reproducible signal rather than replicate disagreement or sampling noise. Its behaviour matches intuition: ρ approaches 1 when the effect is strong and the replicates agree; ρ approaches 0 when the effect is weak, regardless of consistency, so a region that is reproducibly null is not called reproducible; and ρ stays low when τ² is large even if any single run shows a big effect. The effect is squared because reproducibility concerns signal magnitude relative to the variance components; the sign of the effect is handled separately, by the signed β and by a coherence summary, so that "how big and reproducible" and "in which direction" are never conflated. In estimation, β² is bias-corrected (the naive squared mean is inflated by its own sampling variance), and all variances are guarded so that a null region returns ρ = 0 rather than a divide-by-zero.

Two consequences are worth stating plainly. High β alone does not imply high ρ if τ² is also high. And high coherence — directional agreement among a region's residues — is not the same as high ρ; a region can be directionally clean yet vary in magnitude across replicates. The dengue results below separate these explicitly.

### 2.5 Reading across the hierarchy

STRIDE asks the same question at every level of a fixed, structure-derived hierarchy: residue → secondary structure → motif → domain → chain → protein → complex (in the dengue case study the secondary-structure and motif levels are structurally defined but not independently biologically annotated, so the informative transition is from residue to the first aggregated scale, after which secondary structure, motif, and domain are effectively identical in this annotation). For a given locus this yields a reproducibility profile across scales, and the natural summary is the scale at which the region's ρ becomes high. The reason this matters is a structural property of the coefficient: because the regional aggregation depends on the magnitudes present in a region rather than on which residue carries them, a signal whose leading residue shifts from run to run can still appear reproducible at the regional scale even when the residue-level effect looks irreproducible. Aggregation therefore recovers a reproducible signal precisely in the situation, common in short-replicate MD, where the leading residue is not stationary. This is the counterpart of the observation that motivated the framework: the region is stable even though its top residue is not.

### 2.6 Direction and cross-condition comparison

Reproducibility (from the magnitude-based regional aggregation) and direction (from a signed mean, reported only when a region passes a directional-homogeneity check) are computed separately, so a region can be flagged reproducible but directionally mixed. For comparisons across conditions — here, across serotypes — the unit of replication is the condition, not the residue: STRIDE aggregates within each serotype and then compares, so that positions are not treated as independent samples and cross-serotype statements remain descriptive rather than inferential tests.

### 2.7 The provisional threshold

Converting ρ into a pass/fail resolution decision requires a threshold with a known error rate, obtained by calibration against a null in which no reproducible structure exists. On the present dengue dataset that calibration has not been performed, so the threshold is provisional and uncalibrated. We therefore treat ρ as a continuous score throughout, avoid hard pass/fail language, and — because between-replicate variance is only weakly identifiable from three replicates — treat residue-scale statements as exploratory and place interpretive weight on domain- and region-scale claims.

## 3. Where STRIDE sits relative to existing methods

STRIDE is a downstream reproducibility layer, and it is useful to be precise about what it is not, because much of the surrounding territory is well served by mature tools. Cavity geometry over MD ensembles — volume, shape, and their evolution — is covered by POVME [6,7], MDpocket/fpocket [8,9], Epock [10], and related tools; the dengue pocket volumes used here as the functional observable are exactly this kind of upstream input. The question of which residues cooperatively open a (cryptic) pocket has a strong, well-validated solution in the exposon approach [11] built on Markov state models [13,14] and mutual information of solvent exposure, and cryptic allosteric sites have been characterized in this way [12]; these are the methods of choice when sampling supports an MSM. Residue-interaction networks, generalized correlations, and communication-pathway analysis are provided by dynamical network analysis [15,16] and generalized-correlation measures [17,18], and consolidated across methods by integrative packages [19]. Autocorrelation-aware uncertainty estimation is standard best practice [3,4,5], and the expectation of multiple independent replicates with statistical analysis is by now a community norm rather than an innovation [1,2].

Against that background, STRIDE does not claim new cavity descriptors, a new network engine, or the general principle that replicates should be run. Its intended contribution is narrower and specific: an automated, autocorrelation-aware, replicate-native layer that separates between-replicate from within-replicate variance in a per-region effect field, expresses reproducibility as a single coefficient interpretable at every level of a spatial hierarchy, moves a claim from residue to region to domain when residue-level evidence is unstable, keeps signed effect and directional coherence distinct from reproducibility, performs cross-condition comparison with the condition as the unit of replication, and does so deterministically with schema-validated outputs and a separated figure layer. Individually these ingredients are standard; what is not commonly packaged is their combination into a per-claim, scale-aware reproducibility decision for the low-replicate regime, positioned as complementary to — not a replacement for — geometry, network, and state-model methods, which remain the tools of choice when data permit. STRIDE consumes effect fields; those methods produce them.

## 4. Results

STRIDE produces per-serotype and cross-serotype reduction tables and a publication figure suite. Throughout, ρ is the reproducibility coefficient at the stated scale, β the signed effect, τ² and σ̄² the between- and autocorrelation-corrected within-replicate variance components, and coherence the directional agreement within a region. All results are from three replicate runs per serotype; the threshold is provisional, so ρ is read as a continuous score and residue-scale statements are exploratory.

### 4.1 A comparable analysis substrate across serotypes

The four systems, each simulated in three independent replicate runs (twelve replicate runs in total), were placed on a shared residue axis (NS2B cofactor residues numbered negative, NS3 positive) and reduced to per-serotype reproducibility profiles over the full protease. The serotypes contribute comparable numbers of analysable loci (236, 247, 242, and 199 for DENV1–4; range 199–247) and gated residues at the provisional threshold (122, 137, 145, and 106; range 106–145), and per-serotype median residue-level ρ is closely clustered (0.515, 0.535, 0.558, 0.528 for DENV1–4). No serotype is globally more reproducible than another, and the fraction of directionally mixed mechanisms is similar across serotypes (0.703, 0.680, 0.612, 0.704). These summaries are the substrate for the analyses that follow.

### 4.2 Reproducibility is distributed along NS2B–NS3, not localized to the catalytic core (Figure 1)

The residue-level reproducibility landscape, taken as the median ρ over serotypes along the protease, is heterogeneous and spread across both chains (Figure 1A). Across all residue-level values ρ has a median of 0.537 (interquartile range 0.368–0.710, full range 0.014–0.936), with NS2B and NS3 comparable (chain medians 0.559 and 0.527) and no dominant catalytic peak; the catalytic-triad residues, shown as landmarks, sit at intermediate-to-low ρ rather than at the maxima (median ρ 0.651 for NS3:51, 0.299 for NS3:75, and 0.434 for NS3:135). The architecture track (Figure 1B) shows the triad as three discrete residue positions rather than a contiguous high-ρ block, and much of the reproducible signal lies in the broad regions of both chains that are not assigned to a named structural motif. These data indicate that residue-level reproducibility is a distributed property of the protease and that catalytic residues are unremarkable in reproducibility relative to their functional prominence.

### 4.3 Reproducibility improves upon aggregation and saturates above the residue scale (Figure 2)

Tracking ρ for catalytic-region loci across the residue → secondary-structure → motif → domain hierarchy shows a characteristic jump: ρ is lowest at the residue scale and rises once residues are aggregated, then plateaus, with the secondary-structure, motif, and domain levels collapsing to essentially the same value in this hierarchy (Figure 2A). Mean ρ rises from 0.527 at the residue scale to 0.897 at the first aggregate (secondary structure), where it plateaus — the secondary-structure, motif, and domain levels share the same mean (0.897) and median (0.975). The informative transition is therefore from residue to first aggregate, with little further gain from coarsening. Consistent with this, the scale at which each locus is gated against the provisional threshold remains dominated by the residue and secondary-structure levels across all four serotypes (510 and 399 loci pooled across serotypes, versus 15 gating at the chain scale), with the domain level not appearing as a gated category (Figure 2B). Read together, the panels make a specific point: aggregation recovers reproducibility that is absent at the residue scale, yet many individual loci still gate fine, so the appropriate scale for a claim is an empirical property of the data rather than a modelling choice. In this hierarchy the secondary-structure, motif, and domain levels are structurally defined but not independently annotated with distinct biology, so their collapse to one plateau is expected and is noted as a caveat rather than a finding.

### 4.4 Catalytic domains are directionally coherent but not the highest-ρ regions (Figure 3)

At the domain scale, reproducibility is heterogeneous across regions and does not rank catalytic domains at the top (Figure 3A). Across serotypes, the unassigned NS3 span is the most reproducible domain (domain ρ 0.903–0.978, mean 0.958), while the Catalytic Triad is the lowest-mean-ρ domain of those listed (ρ 0.362–0.778, mean 0.526) and the Oxyanion Loop is also among the least reproducible (ρ 0.565–0.894, mean 0.680). What sets the catalytic domains apart is directional coherence rather than reproducibility (Figure 3B): they occupy the coherent region of the coherence-versus-ρ plane (Catalytic Triad coherence 0.572–0.965, Oxyanion Loop 0.712–0.889) despite their modest ρ, whereas the most reproducible unassigned NS3 span is comparatively low in coherence. The data therefore support the statement that catalytic regions are directionally consistent across replicates even though they are not the most reproducibly resolved regions of the protease — a genuinely mixed result that the figure is built to convey rather than to smooth over.

### 4.5 Statistically robust signed effects are widespread (Figure 4)

Separating the signed effect and its false-discovery-controlled significance from reproducibility shows that significant signed effects are distributed broadly across both chains rather than concentrated in catalytic regions (Figure 4). Of the 924 loci in the per-locus significance screen, 302 carry a signed effect and 152 are FDR-significant. In the volcano view, significant effects populate both signs and span the sequence, and the highest-significance effects are predominantly non-catalytic: of the 152 FDR-significant loci, 14 fall in the Catalytic Triad or Oxyanion Loop domains (3 and 11 respectively) and 138 lie outside them by count, with the large unassigned spans of both chains contributing most of the significant signal. Catalytic residues are highlighted where they are themselves significant, so that their robust effects are visible while it remains clear that they are neither the majority nor the extreme of the significant set. The supported statement is that significant signed effects are widespread, with catalytic examples present but not dominant; this is an absolute-count description and not a formal test of enrichment or depletion.

### 4.6 Cross-serotype reproducibility is position-specific (Figure 5)

Treating serotype as the unit of replication, STRIDE counts for each position how many serotypes independently reproduce it. The distribution of conservation classes spans the full range (18, 49, 100, 63, and 18 positions reproducing in zero, one, two, three, and four serotypes), with most positions reproducing in an intermediate number of serotypes rather than all or none (Figure 5A); the 18 positions reproducible in all four serotypes are non-catalytic (none is a catalytic-triad residue). Per-serotype counts of reproducible positions, split by chain, show that NS3 contributes the majority in every serotype with a smaller NS2B contribution (Figure 5B). Positions reproducible in more serotypes tend to have higher median residue-level ρ, which rises monotonically across the conservation classes (median ρ 0.339, 0.395, 0.529, 0.625, and 0.744 from zero to four reproducing serotypes; Figure 5C). We do not claim that more-conserved positions carry larger signed effects; effect size is kept separate from conservation, and Figure 5 addresses only the conservation–reproducibility relationship. The supported statement is that cross-serotype reproducibility is position-specific and heterogeneous, with a limited shared core, and does not reduce to a single conserved catalytic mechanism.

### 4.7 Between-replicate variance dominates the uncertainty budget (Figure 6)

Decomposing each domain's effect variance into between-replicate (τ²) and autocorrelation-corrected within-replicate (σ̄²) components shows that τ² dominates in nearly every region of every serotype (Figure 6). Across the thirty-two domain-by-serotype entries, the median τ² fraction is 0.925 (interquartile range 0.833–0.963, full range 0.358–0.994). Most entries are replicate-dominated (28 of 32), with two balanced and two sampling-dominated exceptions: the DENV1 120s Loop and the DENV4 B2b-C2 Hairpin are sampling-dominated (τ² fraction 0.361 and 0.358), while the DENV1 and DENV3 Gly45 Turn are balanced (0.594 and 0.425). The catalytic domains are firmly replicate-dominated. These data indicate that reproducibility in this system is limited by disagreement between independent replicates rather than by within-run sampling, which is the regime in which single-trajectory mechanism claims are least reliable and in which additional independent replicates, rather than longer single runs, are the appropriate way to strengthen a claim. This also accounts for the earlier panels: residue-level reproducibility is distributed and modest, and catalytic regions are not maximally reproducible, because the effect fields are limited by between-replicate variance.

### 4.8 Supplementary observations

The per-serotype residue-level ρ landscapes (Supplementary Figure S1) show that the distributed, heterogeneous character of Figure 1 holds within each serotype and is not an artifact of taking a median across serotypes. Placing the signed effects of Figure 4 on the sequence axis (Supplementary Figure S2) confirms that significant signed effects occur throughout NS2B–NS3, with catalytic positions embedded in the field rather than dominating it.

## 5. Discussion

Analysed one replicate at a time, the dengue proteases invite a clean catalytic-localization story. Analysed across replicates and across scales, they tell a more restrained and, we argue, more trustworthy one. Reproducibility is distributed rather than catalytic-core-localized (Figure 1); it emerges upon aggregation and largely saturates above the residue scale (Figure 2); catalytic domains are directionally coherent but not the most reproducible (Figure 3); robust signed effects are widespread (Figure 4); cross-serotype reproducibility is position-specific (Figure 5); and between-replicate variance dominates the uncertainty (Figure 6). None of these individually is exotic, but together they are the picture that a single-trajectory analysis of the same data would have missed or contradicted, because the earlier per-residue drivers of these simulations did not reproduce across runs (Supplementary Note S1).

The three separations built into the framework are what make this picture legible. Reproducibility distinct from effect size is visible in the decoupling between the widespread significant β of Figure 4 and the ρ landscape of Figure 1. Coherence distinct from reproducibility is the content of Figure 3, where catalytic domains are coherent yet modest in ρ. Scale as a variable is Figure 2, where residue-scale irreproducibility is recovered by aggregation. Fused into a single importance score, these would have reproduced the catalytic-localization artifact; kept apart, they yield an honest, mixed account.

For interpretation and design, the implications should be drawn at the regional and reproducibility level. Because between-replicate variance dominates and single-run drivers are unstable, a mechanistic hypothesis for this system — for instance that a particular loop regulates the catalytic pocket — is better stated at the domain or region scale and checked across independent replicates before it is relied upon. The cross-serotype analysis adds that a pan-serotype claim cannot be read off one serotype: reproducibility is position-specific and only a limited core reproduces in all four. For a conserved antiviral target, the operational lesson is that dynamics-based target hypotheses require replicate- and serotype-aware validation, and that the apparent per-serotype mechanism of a single trajectory is not a safe basis for commitment.

We are deliberately conservative about the framework's standing. Its components — variance-components modelling, a reliability-style coefficient, effective-sample-size corrections, Bayesian shrinkage at small replicate counts, region aggregation, and a structural hierarchy — are individually standard, and the surrounding tools for geometry, networks, and state models are mature and, where sampling permits, stronger for their respective questions. What STRIDE adds is the packaging of these ingredients into a per-claim, scale-aware reproducibility decision for the few-replicate regime, together with a case study that shows why such a decision is needed. On this dataset the decision threshold is provisional; calibrating it against a null with a controlled false-resolution rate, and demonstrating the gate on systems with known ground truth beyond dengue, is the step that would convert the present descriptive use into a calibrated procedure. Until then the honest posture is the one taken here: report ρ, β, the variance budget, and cross-serotype conservation with their uncertainty, and let spatial scale — not a calibrated verdict — bound the claims.

## 6. Methods

### 6.1 Upstream simulations and effect field

The dengue effect fields analysed here derive from triplicate all-atom MD of the complete NS2B–NS3 complex for each serotype, with a per-frame catalytic-pocket volume as the functional observable and, for each residue, the signed correlation between that residue's distance to the catalytic centre and the pocket volume as the per-replicate effect. The following simulation details must be specified before quantitative interpretation and are placeholders here: starting structures and PDB accessions [TK]; MD engine and version [TK]; force field [TK]; water model [TK]; ensemble, temperature, pressure, and ionic conditions [TK]; equilibration protocol [TK]; the protocol by which the three replicates were generated (independent velocity seeds or independent builds) [TK]; trajectory length and frame sampling [TK]. The downstream canonical residue table represents 50, 62, 60, and 31 NS2B residues for DENV1-DENV4, respectively; this is a STRIDE output-table fact, not a substitute for upstream model-building provenance. The pocket-volume and directional-coupling computation (inclusion-sphere definition, distance metric, correlation, and multiple-comparison procedures) is summarized in Supplementary Note S1 as prior exploratory analysis; STRIDE consumes the resulting per-replicate effect field and does not itself perform trajectory analysis. Because both the pocket observable and the residue distances are anchored to the catalytic triad, near-anchor effects may include a shared-reference-frame component. We therefore treat a shared-anchor sensitivity analysis — partialling out the shared-frame component and confirming that the regional conclusions are unchanged — as a required control before any biological interpretation of individual near-triad residues.

### 6.2 Per-replicate effect and its uncertainty

For each replicate and residue, the effect is estimated together with its within-replicate sampling variance under temporal autocorrelation, using an effective sample size equal to the number of frames divided by twice the integrated autocorrelation time of the per-frame product/residual series, estimated with automatic windowing [3,5]. A Fisher approximation supplies the sampling variance for a correlation effect, verified by a circular block bootstrap with block length of order twice the integrated autocorrelation time and percentiles taken on the Fisher-z scale, with a stationary-bootstrap option and a Fisher-interval fallback for short series [4]; frames are not treated as independent.

### 6.3 Variance components, reproducibility, signed effect, and significance

For each region at each scale, a regional statistic is formed per replicate by an energy aggregation of the standardized per-residue effects, which depends on the magnitudes present in the region rather than on which residue carries them. This regional statistic is modelled across replicates as a reproducible mean plus a between-replicate term (variance τ²) and a within-replicate term (variance σ̄², propagated from the per-replicate uncertainties). The between-replicate variance is estimated with a Paule–Mandel moment estimator when the replicate count is moderate (five or more) and, in the small-replicate regime that applies here (three replicates), with a deterministic Bayesian fit that places a half-Normal prior on the between-replicate standard deviation and integrates the posterior by one-dimensional quadrature [22]; a closed-form DerSimonian–Laird estimator serves as a fallback and boundary case [21]. On the dengue data the Bayesian small-replicate path is the one used, and the corresponding fits are flagged as heterogeneity-weakly-identified. The reproducibility coefficient is β² divided by the sum of β², τ², and σ̄², with a bias-corrected β² and guards against degenerate (null-region) division. The signed effect and its confidence interval come from the hierarchical fit, so the interval absorbs both variance components; direction is reported from a signed-mean aggregation only when a region passes a directional-homogeneity check, and is otherwise labelled mixed. Signed effects are screened with a two-sided Wald test on β/β_se from the hierarchical fit, where β_se absorbs the between-replicate and autocorrelation-corrected within-replicate variance, and the resulting p-values are corrected within each serotype by the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure [20].

### 6.4 Spatial hierarchy and scale reading

A fixed, structure-derived hierarchy — residue → secondary structure → motif → domain → chain → protein → complex — is supplied a priori and is not selected from the data being tested; in the dengue case study the secondary-structure and motif levels are structurally defined but not independently biologically annotated, so the informative transition is from residue to the first aggregated scale, after which secondary structure, motif, and domain are effectively identical in this annotation. For each locus, ρ is available at every scale on its path to the root. In this dataset the threshold is provisional and uncalibrated, so ρ is evaluated at the residue scale against that provisional threshold and each mechanism is described by the scale tier its evidence supports; these scale labels are descriptive and exploratory rather than calibrated pass/fail decisions, and a calibrated selection of the finest reproducible scale, though defined by the method, is not exercised here. Because the regional aggregation depends on the magnitudes present in a region rather than on which residue carries them, a signal whose leading residue shifts between replicates can still appear reproducible at the regional scale even when it is not reproducible residue-by-residue.

### 6.5 Cross-serotype comparison

Cross-serotype quantities treat serotype as the unit of replication: effects are aggregated within each serotype before serotypes are compared, and per-position counts of reproducing serotypes are reported as descriptive conservation classes rather than as inferential tests, reflecting four biological systems.

### 6.6 Pipeline implementation

The framework is implemented as a sequence of deterministic, schema-driven stages. An ingestion stage discovers a replicate dataset, reads the per-replicate effect fields, and builds canonical typed tables keyed on a chain-qualified residue identity, with the NS2B cofactor placed on a shared axis with NS3. Annotation stages attach the structural hierarchy and biological labels. A per-serotype reduction stage produces the residue landscape, resolution census, domain reproducibility, signed screen, and per-serotype summary. A hierarchy stage produces the reproducibility-versus-scale structure. An uncertainty stage produces the variance-component budget and the false-discovery-controlled signed-effect screen. A cross-serotype stage produces the per-position conservation, the domain-by-serotype reproducibility matrix, and direction concordance. A replicate stage records per-replicate diagnostics where the inputs permit and marks them blocked otherwise. A reporting stage assembles these into figure- and table-ready artifacts with provenance, including the provisional and uncalibrated status of the threshold. Each stage validates the columns it depends on and fails with a typed error rather than coercing silently.

### 6.7 Figure generation and reproducibility

Publication figures are produced by a separate presentation layer that reads the finished tables and does not re-estimate any model statistic or alter a threshold; where a figure summarizes across loci for display (for example, averaging already-computed ρ within a scale) or re-applies the same provisional reproducibility threshold to count positions, it does so only for presentation and introduces no new estimate or threshold beyond those fixed by the analysis. The two visualization aids used (a display-only trend line and a deterministic ordering for heatmap readability) are non-inferential. Region identity is keyed on chain and domain and residue identity on the canonical label, so that a domain label recurring across chains is never merged or averaged. Analysis outputs are deterministic across rebuilds (sorted, rounded, and free of timestamps), the pipeline is covered by unit and integration tests including a multi-chain fixture, and the figures regenerate deterministically from the frozen tables.

## 7. Limitations

The threshold that would turn ρ into a pass/fail resolution decision is not calibrated on this dataset; ρ is used as a continuous descriptive score, and no controlled false-resolution rate is claimed. Calibrating the threshold against a null, and demonstrating the resulting gate on systems with known ground truth beyond dengue, is required before the framework is used as a calibrated decision procedure. With three replicates, between-replicate variance is only weakly identifiable, so residue-scale statements are exploratory and interpretive weight rests on domain- and region-scale claims; the framework's intended operating range is a larger number of replicates, with three replicates licensing regional rather than residue claims. Cross-serotype comparison rests on four biological systems and is descriptive, not a powered statistical test. Between-replicate variance dominates the uncertainty budget in this system, which is the intended finding but also bounds the resolution achievable from the current replicate set. A large fraction of the protease is not assigned to a named structural motif, and reducing this unassigned dominance would require an upstream annotation step rather than a change to the analysis or figures. The upstream effect is a statistical coupling to a triad-anchored observable; STRIDE quantifies its reproducibility and does not establish causation, and the current draft does not yet include the shared-anchor sensitivity analysis described in Methods, which is required before individual near-triad residues are interpreted. Simulation-provenance metadata are incomplete and are marked as placeholders. Finally, generality is demonstrated on a single biological system; transfer of these particular conclusions to other systems is not claimed, and the surrounding geometry, network, and state-model methods remain the appropriate tools for their respective questions when sampling permits.
